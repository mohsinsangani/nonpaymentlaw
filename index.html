Non Payment of Invoice Law UK

A practical guide to recovering unpaid invoices and protecting your business

Every business relies on steady cash flow, and unpaid invoices can quickly create significant challenges. For small companies, a few overdue payments can disrupt payroll, growth plans, or even long-term survival. For larger firms, persistent delays damage efficiency and increase costs. The Non Payment of Invoice Law UK exists to give businesses a clear legal pathway to recover what they are owed. Understanding this law is not just about chasing money - it's about protecting your business's stability and securing the value of the work you've delivered.

What the Law Covers

The rules governing late payment in business transactions allow creditors to charge statutory interest and fixed compensation on overdue invoices. These provisions exist to ensure that suppliers and service providers are not penalised when buyers delay payment. Understanding how and when these rights apply is the first step in effective debt recovery.

When Is an Invoice Overdue?

An invoice becomes legally overdue when it is not paid by the date agreed in the contract or on the invoice itself. If no date is agreed, the default under the Non Payment of Invoice Law UK is a 30-day payment period from the later of invoice receipt or delivery of goods/services. Clear terms reduce ambiguity and make enforcement simpler.

Charging Interest and Compensation

Under the Non Payment of Invoice Law UK, creditors can usually claim statutory interest at a rate set at 8% above the Bank of England base rate, unless a different lawful rate is specified in the contract. In addition to interest, a fixed sum may be claimed to cover the administrative costs of pursuing late payment. These measures help compensate for delays and encourage timely settlement.

Practical Steps Before Legal Action

Legal proceedings should generally be a last resort. The typical pre-action steps are straightforward but essential: send polite reminders soon after the due date, reissue invoices if necessary, and issue a formal Letter Before Action if payment remains outstanding. Offering a realistic repayment plan can also lead to a solution without court involvement. Keep records of every communication-this evidences your attempts to resolve the dispute and supports any later claim.

Formal Legal Remedies

If pre-action communications fail, the Non Payment of Invoice Law UK provides several formal remedies. For smaller debts, the small claims track offers a cost-effective route. For larger sums, county court proceedings can result in a County Court Judgment (CCJ) that legally obliges the debtor to pay. If the debtor is a company and owes a qualifying amount, serving a Statutory Demand can lead to winding-up proceedings if the debt is not settled.

Enforcement Options

Obtaining a judgment is an important milestone, but you may still need to enforce it. Options include instructing enforcement officers or bailiffs to seize assets, applying for charging orders to secure debts against property, or seeking attachment of earnings where appropriate. Each method has costs and suitability considerations, so choose the approach that best matches the debtor's situation.

Preventive Measures

Prevention is always preferable to recovery. To reduce the risk of non-payment, ensure your invoices always state clear payment terms and include late payment clauses. Use written contracts, run basic credit checks on new clients, and maintain accurate records of delivery and correspondence. These habits make claims easier to pursue and discourage late payment in the first place.

Time Limits to Act

Be mindful of limitation periods: in England, Wales and Northern Ireland most contractual claims must be brought within six years of the debt becoming due; in Scotland the period is typically five years. If you delay beyond these limits, your ability to enforce the debt through the courts may be lost.

Conclusion

The Non Payment of Invoice Law UK equips businesses with practical tools: statutory interest, compensation, court processes and enforcement routes. By acting promptly, documenting every step, and maintaining clear contractual terms, you increase your chance of recovering unpaid invoices while preserving business relationships where possible. Strong credit control practices combined with legal awareness form the backbone of a healthy, resilient business.
